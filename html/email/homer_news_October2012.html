HOMER User Story:
HDT Global Uses HOMER to Save Lives

This month we bring you a special feature written by one of our power users, HDT Global, a US military contractor.

HDT chose HOMER Energy as a design support tool for the analysis of HDT’s hybridized microgrid. HOMER was used in the design phase of the microgrid to determine optimal configurations given the size of the generator, the capacity of the PV and battery complement, and the load profiles of a 150-man military camp. HDT was constrained by Commercial Off The Shelf (COTS) components for everything but the control electronics, but those constraints placed bounds on the design parameters, giving focus to the solution.

HDT specializes in the energy and habitat infrastructure of military forward operating bases and similar installations, where small, light, and energy efficient systems support the expeditionary nature of the compound. HDT has been developing an energy efficient family of products designed to meet the goals and mission requirements of the U.S. Department of Defense. HDT leverages the application of renewable energy where possible in order to relieve the logistical pressure of supplying diesel fuel to operate all of the generators used at the base as the primary means of supplying power.

Among HDT’s products is a hybridized microgrid, comprised of a generator, solar panels, batteries, and sophisticated control electronics. These components work together to harvest solar energy and keep the generator off as much as possible. The resultant fuel savings is significant and serves to reduce the need for fuel resupply. It also allows certain operations to be conducted further forward than was previously possible. Although fuel savings is the apparent benefit, the more important achievement is operational effectiveness for military units.

HDT designed the microgrid according to HOMER guidance, and once the system was prototyped, HDT used HOMER with actual field-tested performance data put into the model. Actual performance was compared to predicted performance as a way to corroborate the results of the prototype system. We found a tight correlation between the original HOMER model and the actual prototype, letting us know our development approach was sound and further confirming HOMER’s utility as a front-end Computer Aided Engineering (CAE) design tool.

We spent a year in the field under seasonal conditions conducting tests and system refinements. In the end, HDT achieved a system that cut generator run-time in half from the full-time baseline. We even went 6 weeks over the summer running lights and computers in the field and the generator never turned on once.

HOMER’s strength is in its economic optimization of equipment configurations for utilities, where the cost of fuel (coal, oil, gas, etc.) is a primary driver of the analysis. HDT’s focus was a little different. The cost of diesel fuel delivered in-theater is a concern to taxpayers, but cost alone does not drive decision-making of the U.S. DoD. The hidden economic benefit of using less fuel on the battlefield is that fewer or smaller convoys are needed to transport fuel, which most importantly saves lives of the service members who otherwise must drive the trucks and provide security.

The military will likely never get completely away from fuel-fired generators because of the absolute need for energy surety, but operating a base with more renewables, less fuel, and smaller convoys will certainly enhance operational effectiveness. Thanks to HOMER, HDT is on the right path to achieving that.

HDT Global is widely recognized for its industry-leading production of state-of-the-art, fully integrated deployable solutions, including shelters, generators, heaters, air filtration devices, parachutes, aerial delivery systems, robotics and other engineered technologies, currently used by U.S. and allied military units worldwide, as well as civilian government and commercial customers. HOMER Energy Trains Utility Managers in Micronesia

HOMER Energy teamed up with the North Pacific ACP Renewable Energy and Energy Efficiency Project to train four power utility corporations in Federated States of Micronesia (FSM) in August. The training was based on an assessment of areas where improved understanding of renewable energy is needed. Issues such as integration of renewable energy technologies in the grid, optimisation of the power generation mix, design of power systems for remote off-grid areas, budgeting for replacement of parts rather than fuel, cash flow management, and tariff implications were explored during a very intense week.

The workshop wasjust the beginning of a journey towards better understanding of how to deal with the reality of renewable energy now being more affordable than diesel generation in the Pacific region, and all the implications of how to best account for a cost structure almost exclusively made of capital costs (initial and replacement), and no fuel cost.
