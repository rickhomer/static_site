HOMER Passes 70,000 Downloads,
Prepares to Reach Out to the World
HOMER 3 will have a new look and improved input and output capabilities

As a HOMER user, you are part of a growing global community of people who have considered the options of hybrid power. We recently bypassed the 70,000 download mark, and in coming months we will have doubled the number of HOMER users since the product was commercialized in 2009.

As HOMER Energy moves into its 3rd year of operations, we are preparing for two important events. The first is HOMER 3, the next generation of the software. Changes to HOMER 3 and upcoming releases are discussed in the next article.

The second event are plans for user meetings and in-person training opportunities. HOMER software's roots are in helping communities. Our virtual users group, which now has over 1200 members, is a way for active HOMER users around the world to assist one another. In 2012 and 2013, as remote microgrids move into the mainstream and become a necessity for affordable power, we want to take our "community" out into the world, through a series of user meetings or trainings held at various locations. We are in discussions with various agencies and groups regarding how exactly how these meetings might work.

We need your help. Please take a moment to fill out our interest survey regarding possible meetings and trainings in your region.

HOMER Tip: The Optimal System Type Graph

The HOMER Software’s sensitivity analyses are one of its most powerful tools. The Optimal System Type (OST) graph is the best way to get a quick overview of any HOMER model that has at least two sensitivity variables by showing what system has the lowest net present cost (NPC) for each sensitivity value. 

To illustrate this, we created an OST graph from the Philippine village of Sicud model, which is available from HOMER Energy’s sample files page.  In this example, there are two sensitivity variables (diesel price, from $0.20/L to $1.00/L, and average annual wind speed, from 3 m/s to 7 m/s.).  A sensitivity analysis shows that there are four OSTs for the sensitivity values that in this model:

  • OST 1. Dsl/Battery: This system type has an 8 kW diesel generator and at least one 7 kWh battery.
  • OST 2. PV/Dsl/Battery: similar to OST 1 but also contains some photovoltaic (PV) capacity
  • OST 3. Wind/Dsl/Battery: similar to OST 1 but also contains at least one wind turbine
  • OST 4. Wind/PV/Dsl/Battery: This system contains the diesel, battery, and both a PV array and at least one wind turbine
The diesel and battery system (OST 1) has the lowest overall NPC in the lower left of the OST graph in the figure, where diesel price and average annual wind speed are both low. At diesel prices above $0.62 per liter, PV becomes cost effective. As the average wind speed increases, wind turbines become more cost-effective. After getting an overview of your design space with the OST graph, you can get more details with the Sensitivity Results Table, the Optimization Results table, the Simulation Results tables, and Time Series graphs.
