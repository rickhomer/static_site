HOMER for the Marines
HOMER Energy Contracted by Marine Corps

HOMER Energy is working with the Marine Corps to develop a version of HOMER for use by Marine Expeditionary Forces.

Marine Expeditionary Units are deployed in a wide range of tactical situations. Each situation requires different solutions for power system planning due to variability in power needs, equipment & fuel availability, and logistical constraints.

While the HOMER model delivers much of the functionality required for planning expeditionary power systems, it lacks logistical constraints and components often used by Marines.

HOMER Energy is designing a version of the software that includes equipment types and micropower modules commonly used by the Marine Corps. Another goal is to simplify the HOMER interface for use by Marines in the field, who will usually not have engineering backgrounds.

Specific micropower modules used by the Marine Corps will be added as components that can be easily dropped into the model. Jet fuel will be included as a potential fuel source for diesel generators.

HOMER is a flexible software tool that models a mix of conventional fuels and renewable energy to determine the most cost-effective configuration for each system.

HOMER to Model Ft. Bragg
Fort Bragg is home to over 95,000 military personnel

HOMER Energy has been contracted to build a model of existing electrical and thermal power supply systems for Fort Bragg, one of the largest and busiest military complexes in the world, with a total daily population of over 95,000 active duty and civilian personnel. Such a large military base has huge energy requirements & vulnerabilities.

In 2011, the complex had a peak electrical load of 132 MW, with 40.9 MW minimum load and around 30 MW of critical load. Fort Bragg currently purchases the majority of its electricity from the local grid utility and has 6 central thermal plants to meet the thermal needs.

Fort Bragg is committed to increasing its energy security, and has implemented several initiatives to move towards energy efficiency, renewable energy generation, and smart grid technology. Fort Bragg has contracted HOMER Energy to build a model of the complex’s energy facilities, develop a design for energy generation alternatives, and train the Fort Bragg Energy Team in the use of the HOMER model.

Lead staff from HOMER Energy will visit Fort Bragg to refine and finalize the preliminary model. The initial visit will include an introduction to the HOMER model and a walkthrough of Fort Bragg’s existing power supply facilities.

HOMER Energy will use the refined model to design a series of energy system alternatives for Fort Bragg, including utilization of biomass, photovoltaic, micro-hydro, and combined heat and power components. Finally, HOMER Energy staff will return to Fort Bragg to train members of the Fort Bragg Energy Team on the use of HOMER and the associated Fort Bragg models.
HOMER Tip: Trivial Systems

Trivial systems is a term that we use to describe systems that appear in the Categorized Optimization Results table, but are only there because they contain a very small amount of a component that would otherwise not be cost-effective.  They are never at the top of the list.  They are most common when you include a small battery bank or PV array in a large system where that component is not cost effective.

Categorized Optimization Results Table

The Categorized Optimization Results table in the HOMER software displays the least cost system for each system type (the “category winner”).  See the green box in the figure.  In this example, there are three real (non-trivial) category winners:

  • A system with a 75 kW and a 150 kW generator,
  • A system with the two generators and 1 wind turbine,
  • A system with the two generators, 1 wind turbine, and a battery bank with 24 S4KS25P batteries,

and one trivial system (see red box in the figure):

  • A system with the 2 generators and 1 S4KS25P battery.
If you were to double click the trivial system, you would see that HOMER never uses the battery.  Despite containing a battery, it is really a variant of the generator only system.  The additional cost of a single battery is so low that the system still has a lower net present cost than the remaining two systems.  To see the non-trivial battery-generator system, you would need to view the overall results.
